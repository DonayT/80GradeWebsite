Intro

Welcome to 80 Grade Software! Where we develop software at an 80 Grade quality providing baseball fans with new knowledge and insights.

For those that many not know, our name is a reference to the baseball scouting grade system. This grade system is used to evaluate players based on their performance and potential. Players are graded on a scale of 20-80, a culmination of grades such as speed, power, contact, etc.

We're currently developing a number of bots and tools to help you be the best fan possible. Check out our bots and stats to see what we're working on!

Our Bots

MLB Matchup X Bot

The MLB Matchup X Bot automatically posts daily Major League Baseball lineups. Each lineup conatins the starting pitcher for each team, along with the starting lineups, posts are made within 20 minutes of the official lineup card submissions by the teams.

The bot also provides statistics for each player in the lineup over their last 5 games, then uses our stat, Relative Heat Index (RHI) to grade each player as either hot or cold, shown by the colors red or blue respectively. Pitcher statistics are also provided, but the shown stats are from their last 3 starts. View on GitHub

Our Stats

Relative Heat Index (RHI)

Why do we need RHI?

Relative Heat Index, or RHI, was first divised for the MLB Matchup X Bot. The goal was to create a metric that would allow fans to better understand how well players are performing at a specific moment in time. We have created multiple versions of RHI that mimic the way many modern statistics are calculated, each with their own unique formula and purpose.

The stats could be used by players and coaches, to help determine when streaks occur, which allows for investigations into causes of said streaks. From a fan standpoint, RHI could help individuals predict game outcomes, and assist in identifying players that are due for a breakout game.

How do we calculate RHI?

The RHI is calculated by taking the player's average of their last 5 games, or their last 3 starts if they're a pitcher, and dividing it by their career average. The RHI is then multiplied by 100 to get a percentage.
